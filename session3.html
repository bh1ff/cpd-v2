Session 3 of 6

Keeping Young People Safe Online

Practitioners navigate ethical tensions across public, private, and direct message digital contexts.

📊 Download Slides (.pptx) 📄 Facilitator Guide (.docx)

Learning Objectives (LIA Template)

Aim

Practitioners navigate ethical tensions across public, private, and direct message digital contexts.

Objectives

  • Respond to safeguarding scenarios in three distinct digital contexts
  • Distinguish between system-level safeguards and verbal/policy-level responses
  • Identify the trade-off between control and agency in digital spaces
  • Recognise legal duties that apply regardless of context

Outcomes

  • Know how to: Assess risk across public, private, and DM contexts
  • Understand: Safeguarding is a design decision, not just a policy document
  • Equipped with: A framework for evaluating whether a safeguard serves young people or the organisation
  • Action: Review safeguarding setup for one digital tool you currently use

Session Plan

Structured across five phases (D-A-K-D-C). All activities include tool, timing, and instructions.

DISCOVER0 – 15 min

"What is harm in a digital space — and who is responsible for preventing it?"

Harm online doesn't always look like harm in a room. Sometimes it's invisible. Sometimes the thing designed to keep people safe actually makes things worse.

Harm Mapping8 min🛠 Sticky notes + whiteboard

Two columns on the board: 'Harm I've seen in person' and 'Harm I've seen online'. Everyone adds 2 sticky notes to each. Group similar ones. What's different about the online column?

Who's Responsible?5 min🛠 Mentimeter ranking

Rank who's most responsible for online safety: The platform? The young person? The parent? The youth worker? The government? Show results. Discuss surprises.

ACTIVITY15 – 75 min
Scenario A — Public Space15 min🛠 Kahoot (live demo)

You're running a live Kahoot quiz with 15 young people. Someone's answers are clearly mocking another participant by using their name as joke answers. Everyone can see. What do you do?

  • Read the scenario aloud. Give 2 minutes to think individually.
  • In small groups: what do you do RIGHT NOW?
  • Then: what SYSTEM change would prevent this? (e.g. anonymous mode, moderated answers, remove the leaderboard)
  • Share back. Facilitator notes responses on the board.
Scenario B — Private Group15 min

You run a WhatsApp group for your youth club (12 members, all aged 14-16). One member screenshots a personal message from the group and shares it on their Instagram story. A parent calls you, angry.

  • Same format: think → small group → share back
  • Extra question: Could you have prevented this through group rules or settings? What settings does WhatsApp even have?
  • Facilitator: actually open WhatsApp settings on the projector. Show what's available. It's less than people think.
Scenario C — Direct Message15 min

A young person DMs you on Instagram at 10pm: 'I'm worried about my friend. I think they're going to do something stupid tonight.' What do you do?

  • Think → small group → share back
  • Key tension: personal device, outside work hours, direct message. Where are the boundaries?
  • What does your organisation's policy actually say about this? Does anyone know?
System vs Verbal Response Sort15 min🛠 Cards or whiteboard

Facilitator reads out 10 possible responses to the scenarios. Participants sort them into two columns: 'System response' (a setting, permission, or design choice) vs 'Verbal response' (something you'd say or do in person). Which column has more? Which is stronger?

KEY IDEAS75 – 95 min

Safeguarding by Design, Not Just by Policy

  • Three contexts = three risk profiles: public (anyone can see), private group (members see), DM (two people only)
  • System-level safeguards: permissions, visibility settings, moderation tools, logging, reporting pathways
  • The over-control trap: locking everything down removes agency and pushes behaviour elsewhere
  • Your legal duties don't disappear because you're online — they apply to all three contexts
Policy Check10 min

In pairs, pull up your organisation's safeguarding policy (on your phone or from memory). Does it mention digital? Does it cover all three contexts? Most don't. Note what's missing.

DISCUSSION95 – 150 min
The Control Slider15 min🛠 Whiteboard

Draw a slider on the board: left = 'Total control (locked down)' / right = 'Total freedom (no rules)'. For each scenario, groups place a marker where they'd set the slider. Discuss: what do you gain at each end? What do you lose?

Write the Missing Policy25 min

In small groups, draft 5 bullet points for a 'Digital Safeguarding' addition to your organisation's policy. Must cover: which platforms you'll use, boundary rules for DMs, what to do outside work hours, who to escalate to, and one system-level safeguard you'll set up by default.

Present and Challenge15 min

Each group presents their 5 points. Other groups challenge: 'Is that realistic?' 'What if a young person disagrees?' 'Does that protect the young person or the organisation?'

CLOSING150 – 180 min

"Which safeguards protect young people — and which ones just protect organisations?"

Some rules exist to keep young people safe. Others exist so the organisation can say 'we had a policy'. Knowing the difference matters.

Two Lists10 min

Everyone writes two lists: 'Rules that help young people' and 'Rules that just cover the organisation'. Share one from each list. Pin them on the board.

Exit Ticket5 min

Sticky note: One safeguarding decision you'd make differently now. Hand it in.

Your Takeaway
Review the safeguarding setup on one digital tool you use with young people. Check: does it cover public, private, and DM contexts?
